Comprehensive Number System

An in-depth exploration of number systems and their properties

1. Foundations of Number Systems

1.1 Natural Numbers (ℕ)

The set of natural numbers is the most fundamental number system, used for counting and ordering. In mathematics, there are two conventions:

  • Without zero: ℕ = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, ...} (common in number theory)
  • With zero: ℕ₀ = {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, ...} (common in set theory and computer science)

Axiomatic Definition (Peano Axioms):

  1. 0 is a natural number (or 1, depending on convention)
  2. Every natural number has a successor
  3. 0 is not the successor of any natural number
  4. Different natural numbers have different successors
  5. Axiom of induction: If a set contains 0 and the successor of every number in the set, then the set contains all natural numbers

1.2 Whole Numbers (W)

Whole numbers are the set of natural numbers including zero. This is essentially the same as ℕ₀ in the inclusive definition.

Notation: W = ℕ₀ = {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, ...}

Algebraic Structure: The set of whole numbers forms a commutative monoid under both addition and multiplication, but not a group because additive inverses are missing.

1.3 Integers (ℤ)

The integers extend the natural numbers to include negative numbers, forming the smallest ring containing the natural numbers.

Construction: ℤ can be formally constructed as equivalence classes of pairs of natural numbers (a, b) representing the "difference" a - b.

Algebraic Properties:

  • Additive group: (ℤ, +) is an infinite cyclic group
  • Ring structure: (ℤ, +, ×) is an integral domain
  • Ordered set: ℤ is totally ordered with no upper or lower bound

2. Rational and Real Numbers

2.1 Rational Numbers (ℚ)

Rational numbers are numbers that can be expressed as a ratio of two integers.

Formal Construction: ℚ is the field of fractions of ℤ, constructed as equivalence classes of pairs (a, b) where a ∈ ℤ, b ∈ ℤ\{0} with (a, b) ~ (c, d) if ad = bc.

Properties:

  • Countably infinite (can be put into one-to-one correspondence with ℕ)
  • Dense in ℝ: Between any two real numbers, there exists a rational number
  • Field structure: (ℚ, +, ×) is the smallest ordered field

Theorem: Density of ℚ in ℝ

For any two real numbers x < y, there exists a rational number q such that x < q < y.

Proof Sketch: Use the Archimedean property to find n ∈ ℕ such that n(y - x) > 1, then consider m = ⌊nx⌋ + 1.

2.2 Irrational Numbers (ℝ \ ℚ)

Irrational numbers are real numbers that cannot be expressed as a ratio of integers.

Properties:

  • Uncountably infinite (larger cardinality than ℚ)
  • Examples include algebraic numbers (like √2) and transcendental numbers (like π and e)
  • Equally dense in ℝ as ℚ is

Example: Proof that √2 is irrational

Proof by contradiction:

  1. Assume √2 = a/b where a, b ∈ ℤ with no common factors
  2. Then 2 = a²/b² ⇒ a² = 2b²
  3. Thus a² is even ⇒ a is even (since odd² is odd)
  4. Let a = 2k, then (2k)² = 2b² ⇒ 4k² = 2b² ⇒ 2k² = b²
  5. Thus b² is even ⇒ b is even
  6. But then a and b share factor 2, contradicting our assumption

2.3 Real Numbers (ℝ)

The real numbers form a complete ordered field containing ℚ as a dense subset.

Constructions:

  • Dedekind cuts: A partition of ℚ into two non-empty sets (A, B) where every element of A is less than every element of B
  • Cauchy sequences: Equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences of rational numbers

Key Properties:

  • Completeness: Every non-empty subset of ℝ that is bounded above has a least upper bound
  • Uncountably infinite (cardinality of the continuum)
  • Archimedean property: For any x ∈ ℝ, there exists n ∈ ℕ such that n > x

3. Complex and Hypercomplex Numbers

3.1 Complex Numbers (ℂ)

Complex numbers extend the real numbers by including a solution to the equation x² = -1.

Formal Definition: ℂ = {a + bi | a, b ∈ ℝ} where i² = -1

Algebraic Structure:

  • Field: (ℂ, +, ×) is an algebraically closed field
  • Vector space: ℂ is a 2-dimensional vector space over ℝ
  • Normed division algebra

Theorem: Fundamental Theorem of Algebra

Every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one complex root.

Consequence: ℂ is algebraically closed.

Geometric Interpretation

Complex numbers can be represented on the complex plane (Argand diagram):

  • Rectangular form: z = a + bi
  • Polar form: z = r(cosθ + isinθ) = re^(iθ)
  • Modulus: |z| = √(a² + b²)
  • Argument: arg(z) = θ = atan2(b, a)

3.2 Quaternions (ℍ)

Quaternions extend complex numbers to four dimensions.

Formal Definition: ℍ = {a + bi + cj + dk | a, b, c, d ∈ ℝ} where i² = j² = k² = ijk = -1

Properties:

  • Non-commutative multiplication (e.g., ij = k but ji = -k)
  • Division algebra: Every non-zero quaternion has a multiplicative inverse
  • Used in 3D rotations and computer graphics

3.3 Octonions (𝕆)

Octonions extend quaternions to eight dimensions.

Properties:

  • Non-associative multiplication
  • Alternative algebra: x(xy) = (xx)y and x(yy) = (xy)y
  • Division algebra
  • Applications in string theory and exceptional Lie groups

5. Advanced Topics

5.1 p-adic Numbers (ℚₚ)

An alternative completion of ℚ different from ℝ, based on divisibility by a prime p.

Construction: Completion of ℚ with respect to the p-adic absolute value |·|ₚ

Properties:

  • Ultrametric space: |x + y|ₚ ≤ max(|x|ₚ, |y|ₚ)
  • Used in number theory and algebraic geometry

5.2 Surreal Numbers

A totally ordered field containing all real numbers and infinite/infinitesimal numbers.

Construction: Defined recursively using the concept of "left" and "right" sets

Properties:

  • Proper class (too large to be a set)
  • Contains all ordinal numbers
  • Used in combinatorial game theory

5.3 Cardinal and Ordinal Numbers

Extensions of natural numbers to infinite quantities.

Cardinals: Measure the "size" of sets (ℵ₀, ℵ₁, ...)

Ordinals: Represent order types of well-ordered sets (ω, ω+1, ω², ...)
