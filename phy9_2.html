Advanced Physics: Quantities, Formalism, and Modern Applications

A rigorous treatment of physical quantities, their mathematical foundations, and contemporary applications in theoretical and experimental physics

1. Fundamental Concepts in Physical Quantities

Definition: Physical Quantity

A physical quantity is a property of a material or system that can be quantified by measurement, consisting of a numerical magnitude and a unit. Formally, it can be represented as:

Q = {Q}[Q]

where {Q} is the numerical value and [Q] is the unit. The space of physical quantities forms a vector space over the field of real numbers with additional structure accounting for dimensional analysis.

1.1 Classification of Physical Quantities

Physical quantities can be classified through several complementary schemes:

1.1.1 Algebraic Classification

  • Scalars: Elements of the field ℝ (real numbers) with magnitude only. Transform as 0th-order tensors.
  • Vectors: Elements of a vector space V over ℝ. Transform as 1st-order tensors under coordinate transformations.
  • Tensors: Multilinear maps between vector spaces. Transform according to the tensor transformation law.

1.1.2 Dimensional Classification

The Buckingham π theorem establishes that physical quantities can be expressed in terms of dimensionless groups. The fundamental dimensions form a basis for the space of physical quantities:

[Q] = LaMbTcIdΘeNfJg

where L (length), M (mass), T (time), I (electric current), Θ (temperature), N (amount of substance), and J (luminous intensity) are the base SI dimensions.

1.2 Mathematical Foundations

The rigorous treatment of physical quantities requires advanced mathematical structures:

Theorem: Dimensional Homogeneity

Every physically meaningful equation must be dimensionally homogeneous, meaning the dimensions on both sides must be identical. This follows from the requirement of coordinate independence in physical laws.

Example: Navier-Stokes Equations

The dimensional analysis of the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations:

∂v/∂t + (v·∇)v = -∇p/ρ + ν∇²v

where each term has dimensions of L/T², demonstrating dimensional homogeneity.

2. Advanced Treatment of Scalar and Vector Quantities

2.1 Scalar Fields in Physics

Scalar fields are fundamental in field theory and thermodynamics:

Definition: Scalar Field

A scalar field is a smooth function φ: M → ℝ where M is a differentiable manifold (typically spacetime). In coordinates:

φ(xμ) → φ'(x'μ) = φ(xμ)

under coordinate transformations x → x'.

2.1.1 Important Scalar Quantities

  • Temperature Field: T(x,y,z,t) in heat transfer
  • Higgs Field: Φ(x) in particle physics
  • Entropy: S as a state function in thermodynamics

2.2 Vector Fields and Differential Geometry

Vector quantities require more sophisticated mathematical treatment in advanced physics:

Definition: Vector Field

A vector field is a section of the tangent bundle TM, assigning to each point p ∈ M a vector in TpM. Under coordinate transformations:

Vμ(x) → V'μ(x') = ∂x'μ/∂xν Vν(x)

Theorem: Helmholtz Decomposition

Any sufficiently smooth, rapidly decaying vector field in ℝ³ can be decomposed into curl-free and divergence-free components:

F = -∇Φ + ∇×A

This fundamental result underlies potential theory in electromagnetism and fluid dynamics.

2.2.1 Important Vector Quantities

  • Electromagnetic Vector Potential: Aμ in gauge theory
  • Velocity Field: v(x,t) in fluid mechanics
  • Spin Angular Momentum: S in quantum mechanics

3. Tensor Formulation of Physical Quantities

3.1 Tensor Fields in Physics

Many physical quantities are properly described as tensors:

Definition: Tensor Field

A (k,l)-tensor field is a multilinear map:

T: T*M × ... × T*M × TM × ... × TM → C∞(M)

with k cotangent and l tangent vector arguments. Components transform as:

T'μ1...μkν1...νl = ∂x'μ1/∂xα1 ... ∂x'μk/∂xαk ∂xβ1/∂x'ν1 ... ∂xβl/∂x'νl Tα1...αkβ1...βl

3.2 The Metric Tensor and Physical Quantities

In general relativity, the metric tensor gμν fundamentally changes how we measure physical quantities:

ds² = gμνdxμdxν

This leads to proper definitions of physical quantities in curved spacetime:

  • Proper Time: dτ = √(-gμνdxμdxν/c²) for timelike intervals
  • Energy-Momentum: pμ = gμνpν
  • Covariant Derivative: ∇μVν = ∂μVν + ΓνμλVλ

The fundamental non-commutativity of quantum operators leads to uncertainty relations:

For any two observables A and B:

σAσB ≥ ½|⟨[Â,Ĥ]⟩|

5. Relativistic Formulation of Physical Quantities

5.1 Lorentz Covariance and 4-Vectors

Special relativity requires physical quantities to transform properly under Lorentz transformations:

Definition: 4-Vector

A 4-vector is a quantity Aμ = (A⁰, A¹, A², A³) that transforms under Lorentz transformations Λ as:

A'μ = ΛμνAν

The invariant magnitude is AμAμ = ημνAμAν, where ημν = diag(-1,1,1,1) is the Minkowski metric.

5.2 Relativistic Energy-Momentum Relation

The fundamental relation between energy, momentum, and mass:

pμpμ = -E²/c² + p² = -m²c²

This leads to the famous energy-momentum relation:

E² = (pc)² + (mc²)²

Theorem: Noether's Theorem (Relativistic Form)

For every continuous symmetry of the action, there corresponds a conserved current jμ satisfying ∂μjμ = 0. The associated conserved charge is Q = ∫j⁰d³x.

6. Advanced Applications and Contemporary Research

6.1 Renormalization and Physical Quantities

In quantum field theory, physical quantities require renormalization:

Definition: Renormalized Quantities

The process of renormalization relates bare quantities (subscript 0) to physical quantities through renormalization constants Zi:

φ = Zφ½φ0, m = Zmm0, e = Zee0

where the Z factors absorb divergences, leaving finite, measurable quantities.

6.2 Topological Quantities in Condensed Matter

Modern condensed matter physics uses topological invariants as physical quantities:

  • Chern Number: C = 1/2π ∫BZ F(k) d²k, characterizing quantum Hall systems
  • Z2 Invariant: Distinguishes topological insulators
  • Berry Phase: γ = ∮C A(k)·dk, where A(k) = i⟨un(k)|∇k|un(k)⟩
